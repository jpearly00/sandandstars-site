Planning Your Trip

Your Moab trip, figured out.

When to come, how to get here, what to pack, and how to make the most of a week in canyon country.

Planning a Moab trip takes longer than most people expect. These are the non-tour questions we get asked most — when to come, how to get here, what to pack, where to eat, how to string the week together. Everything below is general Moab guidance. Tour-specific logistics live on each tour page and in the FAQ.

If you haven't picked dates yet, start with the season. If you have dates, skip to packing and logistics. If you're already on your way, jump to food & groceries at the bottom.

When To Come

The Moab seasons, honestly

March through May is peak — wildflowers, perfect hiking temps, long days. It's also the busiest. Book lodging and tours early. Late March to mid-April is the sweet spot if you can get it.

June through August is hot. Triple-digit afternoons are normal. Tours run at sunrise and sunset, not midday. The desert is still beautiful in summer — just on its schedule, not yours. Afternoon thunderstorms are common in July and August (monsoon season) and make the canyons dramatic, but also flash-flood-prone in slot country.

September through early November is the other sweet spot. Summer crowds thin, temperatures drop back into the 70s and 80s, and the light gets long and warm. Our personal favorite window.

Late November through February is the underrated season. Cold mornings, 40s-50s daytime, occasional snow on the red rock that photographs like nothing else. Fewer visitors, no park reservations, and every restaurant has a table. If you don't mind layering up, winter Moab is a quieter, weirder, better experience than most people realize.

Getting Here

How most people arrive

SLC

Salt Lake City (SLC)

4 hours by car. The most common airport. Big airport, lots of flights, easy rental cars. Drive down Highway 6 through Price and Green River.

GJT

Grand Junction, CO (GJT)

1 hour 45 minutes by car. Small airport, fewer flights, but significantly closer. If you can find a flight, take it.

CNY

Canyonlands Field (CNY)

20 minutes from town. SkyWest runs limited service from Denver. Tiny airport. Worth checking but inventory is thin.

DEN

Denver (DEN)

6 hours by car. Big hub, cheap fares, but it's a long drive through the Rockies. Scenic if you have the time.

LAS

Las Vegas (LAS)

6-7 hours by car. Makes sense if Moab is the back half of a Mighty 5 or Grand Circle trip starting in Zion or the Grand Canyon.

PHX

Phoenix (PHX)

8 hours. Only makes sense for a broader Arizona-into-Utah itinerary. Not the move for a Moab-only trip.

What To Pack

Desert basics that actually matter

Forget the rest of the internet's packing lists. Most of it is padding. Here's what you'll actually need for canyon country, especially if you're spending full days outside.

  • Water bottles — bring two per person, minimum 32 oz each. We supply cold water on every tour, but you'll want your own containers for refilling and for non-tour days. Hydration bladders work too.
  • Layers — desert temperature swings are violent. A 40°F sunrise can hit 80°F by noon. Think base layer + mid layer + wind shell you can peel off and stuff in a pack.
  • Closed-toe shoes with real tread — trail runners or light hikers. Sandals are fine around town and for the river; they're bad for slickrock.
  • Wide-brimmed hat — a baseball cap doesn't cover your ears or neck. Real sun coverage matters here more than almost anywhere else in the US.
  • Sunscreen and lip balm with SPF — reapply. The altitude and the reflected light off pale rock do a number even on cloudy days.
  • Sunglasses — polarized if you've got them. Red rock glare is real.
  • A small day pack — 10-20L, big enough for water, a layer, snacks, a camera. Doesn't need to be fancy.
  • A headlamp or small flashlight — for stargazing, and for early-morning or late-evening campground navigation if you're camping.
  • Chapstick with SPF, a bandana or buff, electrolyte tabs — small things that make a big difference on a hot day.

What you don't need: heavy hiking boots (overkill for most Moab trails), a giant camera setup (your phone will do fine), or any of the gear rental places in town unless you're going into Canyonlands backcountry on your own. Our tours provide snacks, drinks, and the rest of what a day in the desert requires.

Food & Groceries

Eating well in Moab

Moab's restaurant scene has quietly gotten very good. There are a handful of places locals actually go, a couple of chains you should skip in favor of the local version, and a surprising amount of good coffee for a town of 5,000.

City Market on Main is the big grocery store and has everything you need. There's a smaller natural foods store (Moonflower) a few blocks south if you want organic or specialty items. Both have decent prepared food counters if you want to grab something for a park picnic.

We send every guest a short pre-trip note with specific restaurant picks based on where they're staying and how long they're in town. It's more useful than a page on a website.

Getting Around

Do you actually need a rental car?

If you're on tours with us every day, no. We pick you up and drop you off at your lodging. If you have any non-tour days — which most people do — yes. Moab is too spread out to rely on ride-shares, and there's no real public transit.

Rental cars are available at all the airports listed above. Nothing fancy required — a standard sedan or small SUV handles every paved road. If you're dreaming of driving Shafer Trail or White Rim on your own, that's a conversation for a separate page, and honestly that's where you want a guided tour anyway.

Gas stations in Moab are normal-priced for rural Utah. Fill up in town before any longer day trip — once you're past the park entrance, you're committed.

Beyond Moab

The Mighty 5 and the Grand Circle

Most of our guests spend 3-5 days in Moab. Some are doing a longer loop — Utah's five national parks (Arches, Canyonlands, Capitol Reef, Bryce, Zion), or the broader Grand Circle that includes the Grand Canyon, Monument Valley, and the northern-Arizona stops in between.

We don't run tours outside the Moab region day-to-day, but our Bespoke Expedition service does handle multi-day Mighty 5 and Grand Circle itineraries when someone wants the whole trip designed and guided. If you're putting a bigger circuit together on your own, the sequencing that works best is roughly: Zion → Bryce → Capitol Reef → Moab (Arches + Canyonlands) → fly out of Grand Junction or Salt Lake. Moab at the end is the right place for it.
